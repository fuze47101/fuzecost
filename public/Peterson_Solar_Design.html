Key Insight: The Pump is the Problem

Your dad's well pump is almost certainly a 3–7.5 HP submersible pulling 15–40+ amps at 240V. Running continuously during summer irrigation, that's 3,600–9,000+ kWh/month from the pump alone. The tiered rate structure means all that pump usage hits the expensive Block 2 rate at $0.1137/kWh. Every kWh we offset with solar avoids this top-tier rate — making the ROI better than the blended average suggests.

3.3 Net Metering / Export Situation

Rocky Mountain Power Net Metering — Important Context

The traditional net metering program (Schedule 135) is closed to new customers. New solar installations fall under Schedule 137 (Net Billing), which pays an export credit rate of only ~$0.056/kWh in summer and ~$0.047/kWh in winter — roughly half the retail rate. This means self-consumption and battery storage are far more valuable than exporting to the grid. Every kWh you store and use yourself is worth $0.10–$0.11 avoided cost, vs. $0.05 if exported. This dramatically favors a battery-backed system design.

Why Hybrid Inverter is Critical

A hybrid inverter serves three roles in one box: solar MPPT conversion, battery charge controller, and grid-interactive inverter with automatic transfer switch. When grid power fails, the Deye switches to battery/solar in under 10 milliseconds — your dad's pump won't even hiccup. In normal operation, it prioritizes self-consumption: solar → loads first, surplus → battery, overflow → grid export. This maximizes the value of every kWh produced given Utah's unfavorable export credit rate.

8. Installation Approach

8.1 Recommended DIY + Licensed Electrician Approach

Given your family's hands-on capability, significant cost can be saved by self-performing the mechanical work and hiring a licensed electrician only for the grid interconnection, panel hookup, and inspection-required work.

Phase 1: Site Prep & Foundations (DIY — 2-3 weekends)

Survey and mark tracker post locations along south and west fenceline. Dig or auger concrete pier holes (12"–16" diameter, 48" deep — below Tooele County frost line). Pour concrete with J-bolts or embedded post brackets. Allow 7 days cure time.

Phase 2: Trenching & Conduit (DIY or rent trencher — 1-2 days)

Trench from array runs to power center location. Lay 2" Schedule 40 PVC conduit with pull rope. Trench depth: 24" minimum. Pull DC home-run wires after tracker installation.

Phase 3: Tracker Assembly & Panel Mount (DIY — 2-3 weekends)

Assemble tracker frames on posts. Mount panels. Connect tracker actuators and controllers. Test tracking movement. Wire panels in series strings (typically 2 panels per tracker in series).

Phase 4: Battery & Inverter Installation (Licensed Electrician — 1-2 days)

Install inverter, battery rack/modules, disconnects, ATS (Tier 3), and monitoring. Connect DC combiner boxes to inverter MPPT inputs. Connect battery communication cables (CAN bus). Wire AC output to main panel.

Phase 5: Inspection & Interconnection (1-2 weeks)

Schedule Tooele County electrical inspection. Submit Rocky Mountain Power interconnection application (Schedule 137). Install utility-required AC disconnect at meter. Receive Permission to Operate (PTO).

Phase 6: Commissioning & Monitoring (1 day)

Power up system. Configure inverter settings (self-consumption priority, battery charge/discharge limits, export limits). Set up Deye Cloud monitoring. Verify tracker alignment and calibration. Test backup/island mode.

9.3 Shipping Strategy

Container Consolidation

For Tier 3, all China-sourced components (54 panels + 30 trackers + batteries + inverter + accessories) fit comfortably in a single 40 ft high-cube container. Estimated freight: $3,000–$4,000 FOB Ningbo/Shanghai to SLC rail yard or Tooele, plus ~$500 trucking/drayage to the property. Total transit: 25–35 days ocean + 3–5 days rail/truck. For Tier 1 or 2, a 20 ft container or LCL (less-than-container-load) consolidation works — add $500–$1,000 to per-unit shipping cost. The sweet spot for shipping economics is Tier 2 or 3 where you fill a container.

10. Important Considerations

US Tariffs on Chinese Solar Equipment (2025–2026)

As of 2025–2026, US tariffs on Chinese solar panels and cells range from 25–50% depending on product classification (Section 201, Section 301, AD/CVD duties). However, several strategies can mitigate this:

1. Southeast Asian assembly: Many Tier-1 Chinese manufacturers (LONGi, Trina, JA) have assembly plants in Vietnam, Cambodia, Thailand, and Malaysia. Panels assembled there may face lower or no AD/CVD duties (though this landscape changes — verify current rates at time of order).

2. Trackers and batteries generally face lower tariff rates than panels/cells (verify HTS codes at time of import).

3. Even with tariffs, direct-sourced pricing is often still 30–50% below US retail distributor pricing.

4. Consult a customs broker before placing orders to confirm current duty rates for your specific HTS classifications.

Federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) — 30%

The Inflation Reduction Act extended the 30% ITC through at least 2032. This applies to the total installed cost including panels, trackers, inverter, batteries, wiring, and installation labor. It's a dollar-for-dollar tax credit (not a deduction). Your dad needs sufficient federal tax liability to claim it — consult his tax advisor. If liability is insufficient in one year, excess credit can be carried forward. The battery storage qualifies for the ITC even as a standalone addition.

Utah State Incentives

Utah's state renewable energy tax credit has been phased out, but the property tax exemption for renewable energy systems is still in effect — the added value of the solar system is exempt from property tax assessment for the first 8 years. This matters on a system worth $45K–$65K. Additionally, check with Tooele County for any local incentives or rebates that may apply.

11. Recommendation

Given the property size, available fenceline, your China sourcing capability, and your dad's prepper sensibility — I'd recommend building the infrastructure for Tier 3 but initially installing Tier 2 equipment. Here's why:

Build the foundation for 30 tracker posts (concrete piers along both fencelines). Build the battery shed to Tier 3 specs (8×12, climate controlled). Install the Tier 3 inverter (Deye 30K — cost difference vs. 20K is only $700–$1,000). Then populate with Tier 2 panels and batteries initially (32 panels, 60 kWh batteries). This gives you 75%+ offset immediately and a clear upgrade path: adding 22 more panels and 140 kWh more batteries later is a weekend project, no new infrastructure needed.

The Smart Path: Build Tier 3 Infrastructure, Populate Tier 2 Equipment

Initial investment: ~$35,000–$40,000 before ITC (~$25,000–$28,000 net)
Immediate savings: ~$2,800/year
Upgrade to full Tier 3 later: ~$15,000–$18,000 additional (panels + batteries only, no infrastructure)
Total if fully built out: ~$43,000–$46,000 net — essentially the same as going straight to Tier 3, but with cash flow flexibility.

12. Next Steps

1. Confirm Pump Specs

Get the well pump nameplate data (HP, voltage, amperage, model). This confirms the load profile and ensures the inverter/battery can handle pump startup surge current (typically 3–5× running amps).

2. Walk the Fenceline

Measure actual available length on south and west fencelines. Note any obstructions, easements, or setback requirements from Mountain Glen Rd. Photograph existing fence posts and terrain.

3. Get Tariff/Customs Guidance

Contact a customs broker to confirm current duty rates on solar panels, trackers, batteries, and inverters from China or Southeast Asian assembly points. This could swing total cost 10–20%.

4. Request Supplier Quotes

Send RFQs to LONGi/JA Solar (panels), tracker manufacturers, Deye (inverter), and battery system integrators. Specify quantities, delivery terms (FOB or CIF SLC), and required certifications.

5. File Rocky Mountain Power Interconnection Application

Start the interconnection process early — engineering review for systems >25 kW can take 4–8 weeks. Apply under Schedule 137 for the full Tier 3 capacity (30 kW) even if initially populating at Tier 2 levels.

6. Engage Licensed Electrician

Find a Tooele County electrician experienced with solar PV interconnection. They'll pull the permit, do the panel/inverter/ATS connections, and be present for inspection. Estimated labor: $2,000–$4,000 depending on tier.
